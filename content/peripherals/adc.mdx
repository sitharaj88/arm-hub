ADCs and DACs are where firmware meets the physical world. The peripheral is rarely the hardest part — most measurement problems are in the analog front end before the ADC pin or the filter after the DAC. But the digital side has its own subtleties: sampling time, reference accuracy, alignment of triggered conversions with PWM edges.

## ADC fundamentals

A SAR (Successive Approximation Register) ADC — the type in essentially every Cortex-M — works in two phases:

1. **Sample**: an internal capacitor charges to the input voltage. The longer the sample time, the lower the source impedance the ADC can tolerate.
2. **Convert**: the SAR algorithm bisects: "Is the voltage > 1/2 VREF? Is it > 1/4 or 3/4? ..." It takes one cycle per bit of resolution.

So a 12-bit ADC takes 12 cycles to convert + N cycles to sample. STM32's typical numbers: 12-bit ADC, 1.5 to 480 cycles sample time, 12.5 cycles conversion → total 14 to 492 cycles. At 36 MHz ADC clock, that's 0.4 to 13 µs per sample.

## Sample time and source impedance

Each input pin and the trace and the source resistor form an RC network. The ADC's input capacitor has to charge through this network. **Higher source impedance → longer sample time needed**.

| Source impedance | Min sample time @ 12-bit |
|------------------|---------------------------|
| 100 Ω            | 3 cycles                  |
| 1 kΩ             | 15 cycles                 |
| 10 kΩ            | 80 cycles                 |
| 100 kΩ           | 480 cycles                |

If your sample time is too short, the ADC measures a voltage between the source and the previous channel's residual — and you get crosstalk between channels. Symptoms: "my temperature reading changes when I touch the unrelated channel".

<Callout type="tip" title="Always buffer high-impedance sources with an op-amp">
Putting a unity-gain op-amp (an "ADC driver") between your sensor and the ADC pin gives the ADC a stiff source. You can then use minimum sample time and get clean cross-channel switching. The op-amp typically costs ~10¢.
</Callout>

## Voltage reference

The ADC measures relative to VREF+. If VREF+ is your VDD (typical on low-end designs), then a VDD that varies from 3.2 V to 3.4 V (battery droop, regulator tolerance) translates directly into ~3% measurement error.

Three strategies:

1. **External precision reference** — REF02, REF195, MAX6126 type chips. ±0.05% reference, gives you the full 12 bits.
2. **Internal reference** with VREF+ tied to VDD, then *measure* the internal reference channel and ratiometrically correct. STM32's internal `VREFINT` is at ~1.21 V with ±10 mV factory calibration values stored in system memory.
3. **Ratiometric sensors** — use the same VDD to power the sensor and as VREF+. Sensor output scales with VDD, ADC measures relative to VDD, errors cancel. Works beautifully for resistive bridges.

## Oversampling — getting more bits

If your signal is slow and you're prepared to spend time, you can oversample and decimate to recover effective resolution:

```
ENOB_gain ≈ log4(N)
```

So 16× oversampling gives ~2 extra bits (12 → 14 ENOB). 256× gives 4 extra bits (12 → 16 ENOB). The newer STM32 H7 and G4 ADCs have *hardware* oversampling — you set a register, and the ADC averages internally before raising the data-ready flag.

The catch: oversampling only helps if there's **noise** in the signal big enough to dither the LSB. A perfectly clean DC signal won't gain anything from oversampling (you'll just average the same value).

## Triggered conversions

A timer can trigger an ADC conversion. This lets you sample at *exactly* the right time relative to other events — typically the centre of the PWM period in motor control, when the current ripple is at its minimum:

```c
ADC1->CR2 |= ADC_CR2_EXTEN_0;                     // trigger on rising edge
ADC1->CR2 |= (TIM1_TRGO_event << ADC_CR2_EXTSEL_Pos);
```

Combined with DMA writing the conversion result to memory, the entire ADC pipeline runs with zero CPU intervention. Your code just reads the most recent sample whenever it needs to.

## Differential vs single-ended

Most modern Cortex-M ADCs support **differential** input mode — one pin minus another, doubling the dynamic range and rejecting common-mode noise. Use this for sensors that produce a differential output (load cells, current-sense amplifiers, RTD bridges).

Single-ended mode measures the pin relative to ground. Common-mode noise (ground bounce, switching transients) is rejected only by your filtering, not by the ADC itself.

## DAC

Less interesting than ADC. The two common Cortex-M DAC architectures:

- **Resistor-string DAC** — for low-speed, e.g. STM32 DAC1 at up to ~1 MSPS. 12-bit, easy to drive, monotonic by construction.
- **Sigma-delta DAC** — for audio applications, high resolution at the cost of bandwidth.

Driving a DAC from DMA gives you a waveform generator:

```c
DAC1->CR = DAC_CR_TEN1 | (4 << DAC_CR_TSEL1_Pos)  // trigger on TIM2_TRGO
         | DAC_CR_DMAEN1 | DAC_CR_EN1;

// DMA writes a sine table into DAC1->DHR12R1 on each TIM2 trigger
// → continuous sinusoidal output, no CPU
```

For audio-quality analog output, external DACs (CS43L22, PCM5102) over I²S give you much better SNR than the on-chip DAC.

## Special channels

Most STM32 ADCs include:

- **Internal temperature sensor** — calibrated, ~25°C accuracy ±5°C.
- **VREFINT** — internal 1.21 V reference, used for ratiometric correction.
- **VBAT** — through a divider, useful for battery monitoring.

The calibration values for the temperature sensor and VREFINT are factory-burned into specific addresses (e.g. `0x1FFF7A2A` for STM32F4) — read them, don't assume the typicals.
