The IMU is the centre of gravity of any drone flight controller. Everything else — the GPS, the barometer, the magnetometer, the optical flow camera — provides slower, supplementary information; the IMU provides the high-rate attitude derivative that the inner control loop consumes. Lose the IMU for 5 ms and the drone tips. Get the IMU's noise floor wrong by 0.1 °/s and the attitude estimate drifts measurably within a minute. This is the most timing-critical, most noise-critical, most calibration-critical sensor subsystem in the entire vehicle.

This page covers the MEMS sensors that make up a modern drone IMU, the sample-rate / filter / fusion choices, and how the firmware on a Cortex-M7 turns 8 kHz worth of raw gyro samples into a reliable attitude quaternion.

## The MEMS gyro — what it measures and what it doesn't

A MEMS gyroscope measures **angular velocity** (degrees or radians per second) around three orthogonal axes. It does *not* directly measure orientation — to know "where is the drone pointing", you integrate angular velocity over time. Integration accumulates error: gyro bias (a small DC offset, typically 0.1–1 °/s) integrates linearly into a heading error of a degree per second of flight. This is why every IMU subsystem also has an accelerometer (provides absolute gravity reference at low frequency) and a magnetometer (provides absolute heading reference, low frequency, magnetically noisy near motors).

The classic combination:

| Sensor | Provides | Time scale |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Gyro | Angular velocity | Microseconds to ~1 s of integration |
| Accel | Gravity vector | Long-term (the average over seconds) |
| Mag | North vector | Long-term, distorted by motors |
| Baro | Altitude / vertical position | Slow (~10 Hz useful), drifts |
| GPS | Position + velocity | ~10 Hz, ~2 m absolute |

The fusion filter weights each sensor by its trust at the relevant frequency: gyro for high-rate change, accel and mag for low-rate correction.

## Why high sample rate matters

A drone propeller turning at 6000 RPM generates vibration at the blade-pass frequency: 100 Hz fundamental, plus harmonics. A 1 kHz gyro samples at the Nyquist limit for the fundamental — too close, aliasing is severe. A 4 kHz or 8 kHz gyro lets the firmware low-pass-filter down to ~250 Hz cleanly, removing the worst of the rotor noise while preserving the bandwidth the control loop needs.

The ICM-42688-P's 8 kHz ODR is enabled by an on-chip oversampling-and-digital-filter architecture: the sensor samples at 32 kHz internally, applies a programmable digital LPF, and decimates to the configured ODR. This decouples the mechanical noise floor from the bus-rate the host sees.

On the SPI bus, an 8 kHz gyro polling pattern from a Cortex-M7:

```c
// 14-byte read every 125 µs
spi_dma_read(gyro_buf, 14);   // chip-select pulse, DMA transfer
// while DMA runs, prior sample is being processed
process_prior_sample();
```

The bus transaction at 20 MHz SPI takes ~7 µs; double-buffered DMA overlaps with processing. The CPU never waits on the bus — it's compute-bound, not IO-bound.

## Sensor calibration

Every MEMS gyro and accelerometer has manufacturing tolerances that must be calibrated out before the data is useful for control:

**Gyro bias.** A small static offset on each axis when the sensor is stationary. Measured by averaging readings during initialisation (drone on ground, motors off). Persistent component stored in non-volatile memory; per-power-on component re-measured at every arm.

**Gyro temperature compensation.** Bias drifts with temperature, typically 0.01–0.05 °/s per °C. Sensor temperature is measured on-chip; firmware fits a 2nd-order polynomial of bias vs T from a factory cal sweep. Run-time correction applied per sample.

**Accelerometer bias and scale.** Six-position calibration — sensor placed in each of six orthogonal orientations, each axis aligned with gravity in turn. Bias and scale-factor matrix fitted to the 6 measurements. Typical scale factor error is 0.5–2%; uncorrected, this corrupts the gravity-vector reference for attitude.

**Magnetometer calibration.** This is the harder one. Magnetometers see "hard iron" offsets (DC, from ferromagnetic components on the drone) and "soft iron" distortions (a scaling matrix, from currents and high-permeability materials reshaping the field). The standard calibration is the "sphere fit" — rotate the drone through all orientations while logging mag readings; the data should form a sphere centred at the origin if uncalibrated bias and scale are zero. Fit a 3-axis ellipsoid; invert for the calibration matrix. Some flight stacks run continuous re-calibration during flight.

**Cross-axis sensitivity.** Misalignment of the sensor's internal MEMS axes relative to its package frame, plus misalignment of the package on the PCB. Typically 0.5–1° per axis — corrected via a 3×3 rotation matrix in firmware.

<Callout type="warn">
A common bring-up trap: forgetting to apply the gyro temperature compensation during warm-up. The drone is sitting on the bench cold; ten minutes into flight, the IMU is at 50 °C and the gyro bias has shifted half a degree per second. Without compensation, the attitude drifts visibly. Always run a 30-second temperature sweep with the IMU bagged in a warming pouch to verify the compensation curve.
</Callout>

## Filtering — the harmonic notch

Motor-induced vibration is the dominant noise source on most drones. The vibration spectrum is concentrated at the motor RPM frequency (the rotational rate, not the blade-pass) and its first few harmonics. A simple low-pass filter at 100 Hz removes the high-end content but lets the fundamental through; the gyro sees the fundamental, the control loop reacts, the motors modulate, the feedback loop amplifies it — "motor whine" or "wobble" in the air.

The modern fix is a **harmonic notch filter** — a series of narrow notches placed at the motor RPM (read from bidirectional DShot telemetry, see [drone ESC](/docs/systems/drone-esc)) and its first 2–3 harmonics, tracking the RPM as it changes. As the motor speeds up, the notches follow.

```
gyro_raw → static LPF (250 Hz) → harmonic_notch(rpm_4_motors × 3 harmonics)
        → output to control loop
```

PX4 calls this "ESC RPM dynamic notch"; Betaflight calls it "Dynamic Filter"; ArduPilot calls it "Harmonic Notch". The implementation is a bank of biquad IIR filters with coefficients updated every few control cycles as RPM changes. A typical implementation runs 4 motors × 3 harmonics × 3 axes = 36 biquads, each ~10 multiplies per sample at 8 kHz — ~3 MFLOPS, easily handled by Cortex-M7's FPU.

## Sensor fusion algorithms

Three approaches, in increasing complexity:

### Complementary filter

The simplest. High-pass the gyro integral, low-pass the accel-derived tilt, sum them.

```
attitude_estimate = alpha × (attitude_estimate + gyro × dt) + (1 - alpha) × accel_tilt
```

Where `alpha = tau / (tau + dt)`, `tau` is the time-constant (~0.5–2 s). Works well in 2D (single-axis pitch/roll), struggles with 3D rotations because adding angles doesn't compose correctly. Used in some simple toys and small racing drones; rare in modern designs.

### Madgwick / Mahony filter

Operate on a quaternion attitude representation. Madgwick (Sebastian Madgwick, PhD thesis 2010) uses gradient descent to drive the estimate toward the gravity-and-magnetic-field references; Mahony uses proportional-integral feedback. Both are ~15 lines of math per axis update, ~5 µs per call on a Cortex-M4. Suitable for any drone where the GPS isn't the primary sensor.

Computational cost: trivial. Tuning: two gains (beta for Madgwick, kp and ki for Mahony). The challenge is choosing the gains for the noise environment.

### Extended Kalman Filter (EKF)

The serious tool. Models a 22- to 24-state vector — quaternion (4), gyro bias (3), accel bias (3), velocity (3), position (3), wind (3), magnetic field (3) — and fuses all sensor inputs with explicit covariance propagation. PX4's `EKF2` and ArduPilot's `EKF3` are both production examples.

Run cost: 500 µs to 2 ms per iteration on Cortex-M7 at 480 MHz, depending on which observations are folded in. Predict step runs at gyro rate (200–250 Hz); correction steps run when a new GPS/accel/mag/baro sample is available.

The EKF gives you, beyond attitude, **gyro bias estimation in flight** — the filter notices that gyro integral + observed accel disagree systematically, attributes the residual to slow bias drift, and continuously updates the bias estimate. This is why a properly-tuned EKF outperforms even a well-calibrated Madgwick: it adapts to thermal bias drift during the flight.

| Algorithm | State size | Compute | Drift in 1 min hover | Drift in 5 min with GPS |
|-----------|-----------|---------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Complementary | 3 angles | < 1 µs | ~5° | n/a |
| Madgwick | 4 (quat) | ~5 µs | ~1° | n/a (no position) |
| Mahony | 4 (quat) + 3 bias | ~6 µs | ~0.5° | n/a |
| EKF (PX4 EKF2) | 24 | ~1 ms | ~0.2° | < 0.5 m position |

## Putting it together — a typical attitude pipeline

```
IMU sample @ 8 kHz:
  raw_gyro, raw_accel = spi_read_imu();
  gyro_T_compensated = temp_comp(raw_gyro, raw_temp);
  gyro_calibrated = cal_matrix * gyro_T_compensated + bias;
  gyro_filtered = harmonic_notch(static_lpf(gyro_calibrated), motor_rpm);
  push_to_inner_loop(gyro_filtered);     // 8 kHz inner PID

EKF update @ 250 Hz:
  predict_state(gyro_filtered, accel_filtered);
  if (new_mag_sample) update_mag(mag);
  if (new_baro_sample) update_baro(baro);
  if (new_gps_sample) update_gps(gps);

Heartbeat @ 50 Hz:
  log_attitude(quat);
  send_telemetry(quat, pos, vel);
```

The inner PID consumes 8 kHz gyro directly; the EKF runs alongside at 250 Hz feeding velocity and position to the outer position loop at 100 Hz. The control hierarchy is rate → attitude → position, with rates increasing as you go inward.

<Callout type="tip">
For a new drone bring-up, instrument every stage of this pipeline with [SWO ITM logging](/docs/tools/swo) at a low sample rate (e.g. every 64 IMU samples = 125 Hz). You want to see the raw gyro magnitude, the post-notch magnitude, and the EKF residuals. When something goes wrong (excessive vibration, attitude drift, lost-of-position) the pipeline log tells you which stage failed before the visual symptom appears.
</Callout>
