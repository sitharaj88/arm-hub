A medical-grade wearable is a small body-worn device that measures physiological signals — heart rate, blood oxygenation (SpO2), single-lead or multi-lead ECG, respiratory rate, body temperature, motion — with sufficient accuracy to inform clinical decisions, while being unobtrusive enough that a patient will wear it. It looks like a consumer fitness tracker but is held to a different standard: signal quality has to be clinical, the regulatory burden is substantial, and the firmware lifecycle is governed by IEC 62304. The Cortex-M inside is the same family as in a smartwatch; the engineering rigour around it is not.

This page covers the analog front-end choices for PPG and ECG, the sampling and DSP that turns raw signals into clinical-quality measurements, the BLE-based connectivity to a paired phone or gateway, the regulatory framework that constrains everything, and the battery-life arithmetic that makes the device wearable for days or weeks rather than hours.

## PPG — photoplethysmography

PPG is the technology in every modern smartwatch heart-rate sensor: an LED illuminates the skin; the photodiode measures the reflected light. As blood pulses through the capillaries with each heartbeat, the absorption changes slightly — the AC component of the photodiode signal is the heart-beat-derived "plethysmogram". By measuring this at two wavelengths (red ~660 nm and infrared ~940 nm), the ratio of pulsation between them yields SpO2 (the Lambert-Beer pulse oximetry equation).

An AFE like the MAX86150 integrates:

- Three LED drivers (red, IR, green; the green is mainly for HR, the red/IR for SpO2).
- Photodiode preamp with selectable gain.
- 18-bit ADC sampling each LED's contribution separately (time-multiplexed: pulse LED1, sample; pulse LED2, sample; ambient sample for dark cancellation).
- I²C interface to the MCU.

Signal challenges:

- **Motion artefact** dominates. A person walking, running, or even gesturing produces optical-modulation an order of magnitude larger than the cardiac signal. Adaptive noise cancellation (using a 3-axis accelerometer as reference) is mandatory.
- **Skin tone variation.** Darker skin absorbs more; LED currents must auto-adjust to keep the photodiode in its linear range.
- **Perfusion** — fingertip PPG has 5x the signal-to-noise of wrist PPG. Wrist-worn designs need every bit of SNR they can extract.
- **Ambient light.** Sun, room lights add DC and 50/60 Hz components. The AFE does ambient-only samples for subtraction.

Signal processing pipeline:

```
Raw 100-400 Hz PPG → Ambient subtraction → DC removal (HPF 0.5 Hz)
  → Bandpass 0.5-4 Hz (cardiac band) → Motion artefact rejection (accelerometer-correlated removal)
  → Peak detection / FFT for HR → Cardiac quality assessment
```

A typical HR algorithm uses Welch's method or autocorrelation in a sliding window, returns "60-200 bpm with confidence X". The confidence is what distinguishes a clinical from a consumer device — clinical devices flag low-confidence intervals rather than report a number you might trust when you shouldn't.

SpO2 uses the red/IR ratio:

```
R = (AC_red / DC_red) / (AC_IR / DC_IR)
SpO2 = lookup_table(R)   // calibrated against arterial blood gas measurement
```

The calibration table comes from clinical trials — humans with measured arterial SpO2 wearing the device. FDA / CE clearance for SpO2 requires this calibration to be documented and validated against a standard.

## ECG — single-lead and multi-lead

ECG measures the electrical activity of the heart directly via skin electrodes. Single-lead (two electrodes) gives a single voltage trace — enough for rhythm classification (sinus, atrial fibrillation, etc.). Multi-lead (typically 3, 5, 6, or the clinical 12) gives spatial information about the heart's electrical axes and is required for ischemia / MI detection.

A wearable patch ECG typically has 2-3 electrodes: one positive, one negative, one reference (driven-right-leg amplifier). The signal at the electrodes is ~1 mV peak with a bandwidth of 0.05-150 Hz for diagnostic quality (and the QRS complex is ~5-30 ms wide, demanding sample rates of at least 200 Hz, ideally 500-1000 Hz).

The AFE (MAX30003, AD8232) provides:

- Instrumentation amplifier with very high CMRR (>100 dB) to reject the 50/60 Hz interference from mains.
- Front-end filter (HPF ~0.05 Hz to remove DC drift, LPF ~150 Hz to anti-alias).
- 24-bit delta-sigma ADC oversampling at 8-32 kHz, decimated to 200-1000 Hz output.
- Lead-off detection — checks if the electrodes are making good skin contact.
- Pace-pulse detection — fast circuitry to identify pacemaker spikes (which would otherwise look like QRS to a software detector).

Software processing:

```
Raw 24-bit ECG @ 250 Hz → Notch 50/60 Hz → Bandpass 0.5-40 Hz (diagnostic) or 5-15 Hz (monitor)
  → Pan-Tompkins QRS detector → Beat classification (normal, supraventricular, ventricular)
  → Rhythm classifier (sinus, AFib, AFlutter, brady, tachy)
```

The QRS detector is classic Pan-Tompkins (1985) for monitoring grade or a neural-network-based classifier for higher accuracy. AFib detection from a wearable is FDA-cleared on multiple consumer products now (Apple Watch, Fitbit Sense, KardiaMobile) — the model has been validated against cardiologist-read 12-lead ECG ground truth in clinical trials.

## MCU choice

The MCU does the DSP, BLE, storage, and supervises the AFEs. Constraints:

- **Sample rate × DSP load**: a 500 Hz ECG with motion-correlated PPG + AFib classifier consumes 5-15% of a Cortex-M4F at 64 MHz.
- **BLE concurrency**: streaming ECG to a phone over BLE in real time needs ~50 kbps throughput, well within BLE 5.0 PHY capability.
- **Battery life**: target average current < 3 mA for a 200 mAh battery to give 3 days; &lt;1 mA for a week. Most of this is the AFE and the LED drivers; the MCU is a fraction.
- **Memory**: store 24 hours of raw ECG at 500 Hz × 16 bits = 86 MB. Internal flash won't fit; either compress aggressively (ECG compresses 5-10:1 with lossless wavelet transforms) and store events of interest only, or include external NOR flash, or stream to phone continuously.
- **Security**: patient data is regulated. On-device encryption (AES-256), secure boot, TLS for cloud upload — all needed.

Popular choices:

| MCU | Strengths |
|-----|-----------|
| Nordic nRF5340 | Dual M33, TrustZone, BLE 5.4, FPU |
| Nordic nRF52840 | Single M4F, mature, lots of reference designs |
| STM32U5 | M33, TrustZone, ultra-low-power, autonomous peripherals |
| Maxim MAX32665 | M4F with ultra-low-power BLE, designed for wearable AFE pairing |
| Ambiq Apollo4 | M4F at 192 MHz with sub-µA sleep, very efficient |
| Renesas RA4W1 / RA6T2 | BLE M4, M33 |

Maxim and Ambiq have specifically targeted wearable medical with feature sets like integrated PMIC + BLE + AFE companion chips that minimise external components.

## Regulatory framework

Going from "it works" to "it can be sold" requires:

**IEC 60601-1 (general electrical safety).** Covers leakage currents, dielectric strength, mechanical integrity, biocompatibility of contact materials. The wearable's housing must not leak more than a few microamps under fault conditions; the battery must not exceed a safe surface temperature.

**IEC 60601-1-2 (EMC).** Emissions and immunity. The device must not interfere with other medical equipment in a clinical environment, and must continue to operate (or fail safely) under typical hospital electromagnetic disturbance.

**ISO 14971 (risk management).** Required process for identifying, evaluating, and mitigating risks. Every hazard ("device falsely reports normal SpO2 during hypoxic event") gets a risk score and a mitigation strategy. Software bugs are first-class risks.

**IEC 62304 (medical device software lifecycle).** The software process standard. Categorises software by safety class (A: no injury risk; B: non-life-threatening injury; C: death or serious injury possible) and prescribes development rigour proportional to class. A Class C wearable (e.g. critical-care monitor) requires unit tests, integration tests, code reviews, traceability from requirements to implementation to test, and formal verification or extensive testing of safety-critical algorithms.

**ISO 13485 (quality management).** The company's quality management system. Covers design controls, change control, supplier management, post-market surveillance. ISO 13485 certification is a prerequisite to selling.

**FDA 21 CFR Part 820 (Quality System Regulation).** US-specific QMS. Equivalent to ISO 13485 but with some additional requirements.

**Cybersecurity (FDA 2023+ premarket guidance).** Wearables that transmit data must demonstrate threat modelling, secure development practices, and a vulnerability disclosure / patching plan. SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) for every third-party component.

| Class | Typical product | Software class | Effort |
|-------|----------------|----------------|--------|
| FDA Class I (low risk) | Optical pulse rate "wellness" device | IEC 62304 Class A | 6 months |
| FDA Class II (moderate) | Wrist-worn pulse oximeter, single-lead ECG | IEC 62304 Class B | 18-30 months |
| FDA Class III (high) | Implantable cardiac monitor | IEC 62304 Class C | 30+ months |

The class affects the entire engineering process. A Class II SaMD (Software as a Medical Device) wearable is not a place to move fast and break things.

<Callout type="warn">
Field-update of a medical device is regulated. An OTA update that changes an algorithm or a safety-critical behaviour requires a new submission (or, at minimum, a documented change-control with risk assessment). You cannot iterate the way a consumer product team does. Plan the firmware architecture to allow security-only updates without changing the regulated algorithm versions.
</Callout>

## Power and form-factor budgeting

A wrist wearable target — 7 days on a single charge with a 250 mAh battery — needs an average current of 250 mAh / (7 × 24 h) ≈ 1.5 mA. Component contributions:

| Component | Average current | Notes |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| MCU (Cortex-M4 + BLE radio) | ~150 µA | Mostly sleep, brief BLE activity |
| AFE (PPG continuous HR) | ~600 µA | LEDs at 20 mA × 25 ms × 1 Hz |
| AFE (ECG on demand) | adds ~500 µA when active | User-initiated, ~5 min/day |
| Display (OLED) | adds ~5 mA when on | &lt;5 min/day in interactive |
| Accelerometer | ~10 µA | Continuously sampling for step counting and motion |
| Buck regulator efficiency | adds ~10% loss | Quiescent ~10 µA |

This budget barely fits. The first thing to drop is "continuous HR" — switching to 5-minute sampling with adaptive on-demand brings PPG average down to ~100 µA. Trade is loss of continuous data; in a clinical use case (Holter-style cardiac monitoring), continuous is the requirement and the battery sets the wear-time.

Battery technology improvements (silicon-anode Li-Po, hybrid Li-Po + thin-film) push capacity-per-volume up; the design budget improves slowly.

## BLE and data flow

The device pairs with a phone (iOS or Android) via BLE GATT. Standard services for some signals:

- **Heart Rate Service (0x180D)** — HR + RR intervals.
- **Pulse Oximeter Service (0x1822)** — SpO2.
- **Body Composition Service (0x181B)** — body fat, lean mass.
- **Custom services** for ECG, motion, raw signals.

The phone app stores data, syncs to a backend, applies higher-level analytics, and presents to the user or clinician. Modern apps also support FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) for integration with electronic health records.

For continuous medical-grade transmission, BLE alone is insufficient — it requires the phone to be in range. Premium products add LTE-M directly to the wearable for cellular fallback, at the cost of significant battery drain (cellular TX is 10-100x BLE).
