The rest of armhub treats topics one at a time: a peripheral, a core, an RTOS. This section flips that orientation — we pick a finished product and walk it backwards into the silicon and firmware that make it work.

The goal is *grounded*. When you read about Cortex-A53, you can come here to see one of the millions of devices it actually ships in. When you read about hard real-time, you can see why a drone flight controller picks one core architecture over another.

## What each page covers

- **At-a-glance specs** — chip class, RAM/flash, OS, peripherals, certifications.
- **Block diagram** — the silicon you'd see if you decap'd the SoC.
- **What the cores do** — every CPU/coprocessor and what it owns.
- **Software stack** — bootloader, OS/RTOS, key middleware.
- **The hard parts** — failure modes, certifications, non-obvious tradeoffs.
- **Real products** — concrete examples you can buy and tear down.

## How to read these

Each page assumes you've read the relevant background pages — but you can absolutely jump straight in. Cross-references are everywhere, and you'll often find that *one device touches half the rest of the site*: a Wi-Fi router uses [Cortex-A](/docs/cortex-a/overview/), [Linux boot flow](/docs/cortex-a/boot-and-exception-levels/), [DMA](/docs/peripherals/dma/), [USB](/docs/peripherals/usb/), and [vendor SoCs](/docs/vendors/nxp/).

A flight controller touches [Cortex-M7](/docs/cortex-m/m7/), [SPI](/docs/peripherals/spi/), [I²C](/docs/peripherals/i2c/), [DMA cache coherency](/docs/peripherals/dma/), [Rust on Cortex-M](/docs/programming/rust/), [FreeRTOS](/docs/rtos/freertos/), and [linker scripts](/docs/programming/linker-scripts/) for ITCM placement.

That's the point of this section — to make the rest of the site useful in context.
